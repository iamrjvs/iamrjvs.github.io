Here, information design gets the ultimate upgrade in this design-fueled adaptation of varied elements on the Periodic Table.

Earth’s Alchemy was created for anybody with an interest in learning a little something special about Earth’s elements, in style.
Here, one item from each group on the Periodic Table is featured—that’s 18 for you rookies—along with a description that’s just short enough you’ll actually want to read it.

Every element is accompanied by a handsome, high quality rendering. And for the record, I took some creative liberties when making them, because style matters.
This site is for entertainment purposes, and shouldn’t be relied on as a factual or scientific reference.

Atomic #4

Beryllium

Be·ryl·li·um | Its versatility renders this element essential. With a steel grey color, beryllium assists in the making of copper tools that don’t make sparks when they strike. It’s used in lightweight spacecraft, rockets, and even gems like emerald and aquamarine contain it. It’s like the Swiss Army knife of the Periodic Table.

Atomic #5

Boron

Bo·ron | Masters of science agree; this element rarely occurs in Earth’s crust, nor the solar system for that matter. It’s a mix of metal and nonmetal, is found in small amounts inside meteoroids (that means metallic space rocks) but untreated, isn’t found naturally on Earth. Much of boron is used in making fiberglass, ceramics, and Borosilicate glassware, which you probably know by its common name, Pyrex.

Atomic #6

Carbon

Car·bon | After oxygen, this is the stuff we’re all mostly made of. The way atoms inside carbon arrange themselves determines whether that thing is opaque and transferrable like lead, or transparent and rock hard, like a diamond. It makes both, and so much more.

Atomic #16

Sulfur

Sul·fur | One whiff and you know exactly what it is. Compounds in this nostril-assaulting element are responsible for the characteristically rank smell of natural gas, skunks, and rotten eggs. Elemental sulfur though, is bright yellow, crystalline, and solid at room temperature. It’s mainly used in the production of other chemicals like matches and fungicides. When burned, sulfur melts to a blood colored liquid and emits a vivid blue flame.

Atomic #22

Titanium

Ti·ta·ni·um | The science has long been settled. This shiny transition metal is silver, low density, high strength, and corrosion resistant. It’s found in almost all living things, water, rock, and soil. It’s completely non-toxic and used in everything from heavy duty military scenarios to consumer goods like jewelry, eyeglass frames, and medical implants. This is the stuff heroes, and hip replacements, are made of.

Atomic #27

Cobalt

Co·balt | Natural cobalt is a silvery white metal, with a slightly reddish tinge. It’s found in Earth's crust only in chemically combined form, expect for small deposits in meteoric iron. And as any artisan will tell you, the compounds within it that give glass, ceramics, paints and varnishes their classic blue color is hard to beat.

Atomic #29

Copper

Cop·per | The fact is, copper is one of the few metals that occurs in nature with a natural color that isn’t grey or silver, and, in a directly usable form. Pure copper is pliant, orangeish red and is used largely in electrical settings, roofing, plumbing, and industrial machinery.

Atomic #35

Bromine

Bro·mine | At room temperature bromine is a burnt umber colored liquid but evaporates at the ready to form a highly reactive gas. While it doesn’t occur freely in nature, bromine in varying forms is used in the study of drugs and the treatment of water. Bromine’s biggest use is as an ingredient in flame suppressants.

Atomic #36

Krypton

Kryp·ton | This is the stuff real science is made of, not Superman’s home planet. It’s not green, rocky, or crystal. It’s a colorless, odorless gas occurring in trace amounts in the atmosphere. Neon signs, painted to achieve a desired outcome, are filled with krypton because of its bright white emissive characteristics. Ok? Alright, it’s settled then.

Atomic #39

Yttrium

Yt·tri·um | Lunar rocks have been known to contain it. It’s never found in Earth's crust as a free element. Nearly every living organism (including people) carries it inside them, even though it has no known biological role; yttrium, as mysterious as it may seem, is a silver colored metal that when alloyed can create needles so incredible they cut more accurately than scalpels.

Atomic #44

Ruthenium

Ru·the·ni·um | The laws of science reduce this metal to limited industrial applications because of its fragility and incredibly high melting point; about 4,200°F. It can’t be readily cast, rolled, or made into wire. Ruthenium is a whiteish silver metal used mostly to make other metals tougher.

Atomic #46

Palladium

Pal·la·di·um | From concert flutes to catalytic converters this non-toxic metal is the driving force behind umpteen industries. Palladium is silvery white and part of a group of metals called PGMs, which stands for Platinum Group Metals. The difference is, Palladium has the lowest melting point, which makes it objectively cooler.

Atomic #55

Cesium

Ce·si·um | This soft, gold-colored metal, liquified at a balmy 83°F, is used in atomic clocks which are heralded as the most accurate manifestation of a unit ever achieved. Call it cesium. Better yet, call it the gold standard.

Atomic #73

Tantalum

Tan·ta·lum | This shape-shifting gray metal has a blue tinge, is ultra corrosion resistant, non-irritating, and non-magnetic. That’s why it’s used as a substitute for platinum, even inside the body as coatings on implants, and generously, as a metal powder in the manufacture of electronics.

Atomic #74

Tungsten

Tung·sten | Taking temperature to the nth degree, this steel grey colored metal (multi-colored when oxidized) has the highest melting point of all the elements, 6,192°F. It’s crumbly by itself but when mixed with other metals is used in intensely hot scenarios like arc welding and inside furnaces. And as for its atomic symbol “W”, it inherited that from its former name, Wolfram.

Atomic #75

Rhenium

Rhe·ni·um | The tiniest amount goes a long way. As little as 3% is needed when combined with other metals to make superalloys, which are then used to make jet engines. It’s silver colored and has a boiling point at a scalding 10,170°F.

Atomic #80

Mercury

Mer·cu·ry | This beefed-up, melted-down metal has an insidious track record. It’s been known to make its way into the food chain via fish, and even worse, has been used purposefully in thermometers and batteries. Not good, because Mercury is toxic, especially in large doses. Now, it’s only used mostly in manufacturing and scientific study.

Atomic #83

Bismuth

Bis·muth | This fairytale looking metal has a silver-pink hue but oxidizes to an iridescent finish. In a time long ago, it was used to cast printmaking type. Now, it’s used in cosmetics, and pharmaceuticals. When burned in oxygen, bismuth emits a blue flame and yellow fumes. What could be more magical than that?
